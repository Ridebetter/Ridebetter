Help Centre

Preparing for your CBT

Bring your valid UK provisional or full driving licence. EU licence holders must bring their licence photocard along with a returned D9 form. If you're using your own motorcycle, ensure it has insurance, an MOT, valid road tax and L‑plates. Plan your journey in advance, check your training location, date and time, and arrive early — missing the start of training means you may not be allowed to participate.

What to wear

Wear protective clothing: a thick jacket and trousers such as leather or heavy denim with no rips. Sturdy boots that cover your ankles are mandatory. Dress for the weather — lighter layers in summer and warm, waterproof layers in winter. Most schools provide helmets, high‑visibility vests and gloves, but check your booking confirmation as availability varies.

Course structure

CBT courses cover five elements: an introduction and eye test, on‑site training covering vehicle controls and basic maintenance checks, on‑site riding to practise manoeuvres and gear changes, an on‑road theory session focusing on safe and legal road riding, and an on‑road ride of at least two hours.

Tips for the day

The CBT typically lasts six to eight hours, so it can be tiring. Get a good night’s sleep and eat a proper breakfast beforehand. It’s normal to be asked to return for a second day if your instructor feels you need more practice — this helps ensure you’re confident and safe on the road.

Manual vs. automatic bikes

Manual motorcycles give you full control over gear changes and provide an engaging ride with efficient acceleration; they’re also generally cheaper to buy and maintain. However, learning to use the clutch and gears takes practice and can be tiring in stop‑and‑go traffic. Automatic motorcycles are easier to ride, particularly for commuting and beginners, and eliminate the risk of stalling. They do tend to cost more, weigh more and there are fewer models available. Choose the type that best matches your riding style and future plans.
